Angry

Use a lower, more guttural tone of voice. When you're angry, your voice naturally becomes lower and more guttural. This is because the muscles in your throat and larynx tighten, which lowers the pitch of your voice.

Speak more slowly and deliberately. When you're angry, you may be tempted to speak quickly and forcefully. However, this can actually make you sound more aggressive and less in control. Instead, try to speak slowly and deliberately, which will help you to project a sense of anger and authority.

Use harsh consonants. Harsh consonants, such as "k" and "t," can add an extra edge to your voice when you're angry. Try to emphasize these consonants when you're speaking, and you'll sound even angrier.

Use facial expressions and body language. Your facial expressions and body language can also help you to sound angry. Scowl, clench your fists, and point your finger for added effect.

Calm

Breathe deeply. This is the most important thing you can do to sound calm and relaxed. Take slow, deep breaths from your diaphragm, filling your lungs completely. This will help to oxygenate your blood and slow your heart rate.

Relax your body. As you breathe deeply, focus on relaxing your body. Start with your shoulders, then your neck, and then your face. Let all of the tension flow out of your body.

Speak slowly and deliberately. When you're feeling calm, your voice will naturally slow down and become more deliberate. This will help you to project a sense of calm and authority.

Use a soothing tone of voice. Avoid using a high-pitched or squeaky voice, as this can sound nervous or anxious. Instead, use a low, soothing tone that will help to put your listener at ease.

Disgusted

Use your facial expressions. Your facial expressions can help to convey the emotion of disgust even if your voice doesn't quite match. Make sure to scrunch up your nose, wrinkle your forehead, and widen your eyes. Use your body language.

Change your tone of voice. When you're disgusted, your voice will naturally become lower, more guttural, and more nasal. You may also want to add some harsh consonants, such as "k" and "t."

Use vocal fry. Vocal fry is a speech pattern that is characterized by a low, raspy voice. It can be a very effective way to convey disgust.

Use pauses. Pausing can be a very effective way to emphasize the disgust in your voice. It can also give you a chance to collect your thoughts and to build up the tension.

Neutral

Use a mid-range tone of voice. Avoid using a high-pitched or low-pitched voice, as this can sound either excited or bored. Instead, try to speak in a mid-range tone that is neither too excited nor too bored.

Speak at a moderate pace. Don't speak too quickly or too slowly, as this can make you sound either nervous or uninterested. Instead, try to speak at a moderate pace that is easy to understand.

Use clear enunciation. Make sure that you pronounce your words clearly and distinctly. This will help you to sound more neutral and less regional.

Avoid using slang or regionalisms. Slang and regionalisms can make you sound less neutral. Instead, try to use standard English that is understood by people from all over the world.

Use a neutral facial expression. Avoid smiling or frowning, as this can make you sound either happy or sad. Instead, try to keep your facial expression neutral.

Sad

Use a lower, more gentle tone of voice. When you're sad, your voice naturally becomes lower and more gentle. This is because the muscles in your throat and larynx relax, which lowers the pitch of your voice.

Speak more slowly and softly. When you're sad, you may be tempted to speak quickly and forcefully. However, this can actually make you sound more angry or frustrated. Instead, try to speak slowly and softly, which will help you to project a sense of sadness and vulnerability.

Use breathy sounds. When you're sad, you may breathe more shallowly and your voice may become more breathy. This is because your body is trying to conserve energy. Try to incorporate some breathy sounds into your speech, such as "um" and "ah."

Use facial expressions and body language. Your facial expressions and body language can also help you to sound sad. Frown, lower your gaze, and slump your shoulders for added effect.

Surprised

Use a higher, more pitched tone of voice. When you're surprised, your voice naturally becomes higher and more pitched. This is because the muscles in your throat and larynx tense, which raises the pitch of your voice.

Speak more quickly and suddenly. When you're surprised, you may be tempted to speak slowly and deliberately. However, this can actually make you sound more confused or hesitant. Instead, try to speak more quickly and suddenly, which will help you to project a sense of surprise and excitement.

Use exclamations. Exclamations, such as "Oh!", "Wow!", and "What?!" can be a very effective way to express surprise. They can also help to emphasize your surprise and to get your listener's attention.

Use facial expressions and body language. Your facial expressions and body language can also help you to sound surprised. Open your eyes wide, raise your eyebrows, and gasp for added effect.

Happy

Use a higher, more pitched tone of voice. When you're happy, your voice naturally becomes higher and more pitched. This is because the muscles in your throat and larynx tense, which raises the pitch of your voice.

Speak more quickly and animatedly. When you're happy, you may be tempted to speak slowly and deliberately. However, this can actually make you sound more bored or uninterested. Instead, try to speak more quickly and animatedly, which will help you to project a sense of happiness and excitement.

Use exclamations. Exclamations, such as "Yay!", "Woo!", and "Hurray!" can be a very effective way to express happiness. They can also help to emphasize your happiness and to get your listener's attention.

Use facial expressions and body language. Your facial expressions and body language can also help you to sound happy. Smile, raise your eyebrows, and nod your head for added effect.

Fearful

Use a lower, more breathy tone of voice. When you're fearful, your voice naturally becomes lower and more breathy. This is because the muscles in your throat and larynx relax, which lowers the pitch of your voice and makes it sound more breathy.

Speak more slowly and hesitantly. When you're fearful, you may be tempted to speak quickly and forcefully. However, this can actually make you sound more angry or aggressive. Instead, try to speak more slowly and hesitantly, which will help you to project a sense of fear and uncertainty.

Use facial expressions and body language. Your facial expressions and body language can also help you to sound fearful. Frown, widen your eyes, and shrink back for added effect.
